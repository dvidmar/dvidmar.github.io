My Projects

  • What is an Arrhythmia?
  • What Perpetuates Fibrillation?
  • How Can We Utilize Patient Data?
  • Why do Arrhythmias Terminate?

During a normal human heartbeat, an electrical wave of excitation spreads out evenly across cardiac tissue. This electrical signal causes contraction of the heart muscle, resulting in the pumping of blood throughout the body. If this electrical wave becomes mangled, however, the resulting contraction can become substantially altered, leading to what is known as an arrhythmia. The most serious type of arrhythmia, known as fibrillation, arises when the heart's contraction becomes entirely uncoordinated and ineffective, with its motion reduced to chaotic quivering. My research focuses on understanding the complex dynamical state underlying human fibrillation by exploring the relationship between theoretical principles, computational simulations, and clinical data.

Normal Conduction
Arrhythmic Conduction

Images by wikipedia user JHeuser, distributed under CC BY-SA 3.0

Though fibrillation in humans can exist indefinitely, it is most commonly seen in episodes which last from minutes to days, eventually terminating back to normal sinus rhythm. An obvious question, then, arises: what is the mechanism allowing for the chaos inherent to fibrillation to persist for extended periods of time? Put another way, what exactly is perpuating this arrhythmia and how can we stop it?


Recent findings point towards the importance of consistent rotational patterns of conduction, or rotors, as playing an important role in the maintanence of this arrhythmia. These rotors are grounded in extensive theoretical and computational studies of the heart and are hypothesized to act as the drivers of disorganization. A sample simulation of this hypothesis is shown to the right, where a central rotor exists with wavefronts breaking up far from the core.

Simulation of a rotor surrounded by breakup, red is excited tissue and blue is resting tissue.

To better understand the role that rotors play in human fibrillation, it becomes necessary to collect and analyze clinical data from patients. This is achieved through use of a basket catheter, which records electrical signals at various points on the surface of the heart during an episode of arrhythmia. These signals are then processed and used to create a patient-specific video of the underyling electrical activity. One focus of my work was to create and implement a methodology which was capable of taking in these videos and marking a continuous vector field to identify the flow of electrical activation as well as an automated measure of the rotational activity. With this measure we can effectively track the evolution of rotor activity in each episode of arrhythmia that we record, allowing for systematic studies on rotor dynamics.

An activation map of a clinical episode of fibrillation, where the flow field and corresponding map of rotational activity reveal a dual rotor conduction pattern.
